# History
The "Martinez cocktail" is a classic drink widely considered the direct precursor to the modern Martini, representing a significant step in the evolution of 19th-century cocktail culture. It is revered by cocktail historians and bartenders as a "living artifact" that showcases the balance and flavors of pre-Prohibition drinks.

The exact origin of the Martinez is debated, but its first appearance in print was in the late 1800s. Recipes for the Martinez first appeared in O.H. Byron's 1884 "Modern Bartender's Guide" and Jerry Thomas's 1887 "Bartender's Guide." One popular account suggests that bartender Jerry Thomas created the drink at the Occidental Hotel, possibly for a traveler heading to Martinez, California, and named it after the city. Another theory posits that the cocktail was named after the city of Martinez, California, or was created by a bartender named Richelieue in a saloon there around 1870.

The original Martinez cocktail typically combined Old Tom gin (a sweeter, richer gin), sweet vermouth, maraschino liqueur, and orange bitters, often garnished with a lemon twist. This particular recipe features Dry Vermouth, Orange Bitters, Triple Sec, and Gin. It is a variation that uses dry vermouth instead of sweet vermouth, and triple sec instead of maraschino liqueur, creating a drier and more citrus-forward interpretation of the classic Martinez.
